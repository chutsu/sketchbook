Differential Calculus

Lie Group $\mathrm{SO}(3)$

  • Not a vector space
  • Has no addition operator
  • Has no subtraction operator

State estimation frameworks rely on small differences and gradients in order to

correct the state estimate. Orientations unlike translation and velocity do not

have an addition operator, as such it is more involving to update or find the

gradients of orientations. Forunately, since orientations are a special

orhogonal group $\mathrm{SO}(3)$ as well as a Lie group, an exponential map

exists that relates to its Lie algebra allowing orientations to be perturbed

and its gradients calculated.

Elements in Lie algebra are abstract vectors and not suitable for actual

computations. A basis $\mat{B} = [\vec{\boldsymbol{\varphi}}_{1} \enspace \vec{\boldsymbol{\varphi}}_{2} \enspace \vec{\boldsymbol{\varphi}}_{3}]$ can be used to extend the map to

$\real^{3}$.

The definition of an exponential map $exp : \real^{3} \mapsto \mathrm{SO}(3)$ of a coordinate tuple $\boldsymbol{\varphi} (\varphi_1, \varphi_2, \varphi_3) \in \real^{3}$ is defined by

$$ \text{exp}(\boldsymbol{\varphi}) := Exp( \vec{\boldsymbol{\varphi}}_{1}\varphi_{1}, \vec{\boldsymbol{\varphi}}_{2}\varphi_{2}, \vec{\boldsymbol{\varphi}}_{3}\varphi_{3} ) $$ $$ Exp((t + s) \vec{\boldsymbol{\varphi}}) = Exp(t\vec{\boldsymbol{\varphi}}) \circ Exp(s\vec{\boldsymbol{\varphi}}) \qquad \forall t, s \in \real, \forall \vec{\boldsymbol{\varphi}} \in $$ $$ \boxplus :& \mathrm{SO}(3) \times \real^{3} \rightarrow \mathrm{SO}(3), \\ &\Phi, \boldsymbol{\varphi} \mapsto \text{exp}(\boldsymbol{\varphi}) \circ \Phi, \\ \boxminus :& \mathrm{SO}(3) \times \mathrm{SO}(3) \rightarrow \real^{3}, \\ &\Phi_1, \Phi_2 \mapsto \text{log}(\Phi_1 \circ \Phi_{2}^{-1}) $$

Similar to regular addition and subtraction, both operators have the following

identities,

$$ \Phi \boxplus \vec{0} &= \Phi \\ (\Phi \boxplus \boldsymbol{\varphi}) \boxminus \Phi &= \boldsymbol{\varphi} \\ \Phi_1 \boxplus (\Phi_2 \boxminus \Phi_1) &= \Phi_2 $$

Special Orthogonal Group $\mathrm{SO}(3)$

Special Orthogonal Group $\mathrm{SO}(3)$ describes the group of 3D rotation

matrices and it is formally defined as $\mathrm{SO}(3) \dot{=} \{ \rot \in \real^{3 \times 3} : \rot^{{T}} \rot = \eye, \det{\rot} = 1 \}$ . The

group operation is the usual matrix multiplication, and the inverse is the

matrix transpose. The group $\mathrm{SO}(3)$ also forms a smooth manifold. The

tangent space to the manifold (at the identity) is denoted $\mathfrak{so}(3)$,

which is also called the Lie algebra and coincides with the space of `3

\times 3` skew symmetric matrices.

$$ \angvel \in \real^{3}, \enspace \vee{\angvel} = \begin{bmatrix} 0 & -\omega_{3} & \omega_{2} \\ \omega_{3} & 0 & -\omega_{1} \\ -\omega_{2} & \omega_{1} & 0 \end{bmatrix} $$ $$ \text{Exp}: \real^{3} \rightarrow \mathrm{SO}(3) ; \enspace \boldsymbol{\phi} \rightarrow \text{exp}(\vee{\boldsymbol{\phi}}) \\ \text{Log}: \mathrm{SO}(3) \rightarrow \real^{3} ; \enspace \rot \rightarrow \vee{\text{log}(\rot)} $$

Exponential Map

The exponential map (at the identity) $exp: \mathfrak{so}(3) \rightarrow \mathrm{SO}(3)$ associates an element of the Lie Algebra to a rotation:

$$ \text{exp}(\vee{\boldsymbol{\phi}}) = \eye + \dfrac{\sin(|| \boldsymbol{\phi} ||)} {|| \boldsymbol{\phi} ||} \vee{\boldsymbol{\phi}} + \dfrac{1 - \cos(|| \boldsymbol{\phi} ||)} {|| \boldsymbol{\phi} ||^{2}} \vee{\boldsymbol{\phi}}^{2} $$

Logarithmic Map

The logarithmic map (at the identity) associates rotation matrix

$\rot \in \mathrm{SO}(3)$ to a skew symmetric matrix:

$$ \log(\rot) = \dfrac{\psi \cdot (\rot - \rot^{{T}})}{2 \sin(\psi)}, \enspace \psi = \cos^{-1} \left( \dfrac{\tr{\rot) - 1}}{2} \right) $$

Properties

$$ \begin{align} \rot \text{Exp}(\boldsymbol{\phi}) \rot^{{T}} &= \text{Exp}(\rot \vee{\boldsymbol{\phi}} \rot^{{T}}) \\ &= \text{Exp}(\rot \boldsymbol{\phi}) \\ &= \text{Exp}(\boldsymbol{\phi}) \rot \\ &= \rot^{{T}} \text{Exp}(\boldsymbol{\phi}) \rot \end{align} $$ $$ \text{Exp}(\boldsymbol{\phi} + \delta\boldsymbol{\phi}) \approx \text{Exp}(\boldsymbol{\phi}) \text{Exp}(\mathbf{J}_{r}(\boldsymbol{\phi}) \delta\boldsymbol{\phi}) $$ $$ \text{Log}( \text{Exp}(\boldsymbol{\phi}) \text{Exp}(\delta\boldsymbol{\phi}) ) \approx \boldsymbol{\phi} + \mathbf{J}_{r}^{-1}(\boldsymbol{\phi}) \delta\boldsymbol{\phi} $$ $$ \mathbf{J}_{r}(\boldsymbol{\phi}) = \eye - \dfrac{1 - \cos(||\boldsymbol{\phi}||)}{||\boldsymbol{\phi}||^{2}} + \dfrac{||\boldsymbol{\phi} - \sin(||\boldsymbol{\phi}||)} {||\boldsymbol{\phi^{3}}||} (\vee{\boldsymbol{\phi}})^{2} $$ $$ \mathbf{J}_{r}^{-1}(\boldsymbol{\phi}) = \eye - \dfrac{1}{2} \vee{\boldsymbol{\phi}} + \left( \dfrac{1}{||\boldsymbol{\phi}||^{2}} + \dfrac{1 + \cos(||\boldsymbol{\phi}||} {2 ||\boldsymbol{\phi}||^{2} \sin(||\boldsymbol{\phi}||)} \right) \vee{\boldsymbol{\phi}}^{2} $$
